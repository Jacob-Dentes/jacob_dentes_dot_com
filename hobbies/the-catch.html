The Catch

A short story by Jacob Dentes

Tula coughed. She tasted blood. She swallowed it down. Tula wouldn’t have her show ruined by getting blood on her dress, but she couldn’t stop trying it on. She tugged her sleeves in a fruitless attempt to hide the growing pale splotches on her arms.

The Catch was everything Tula dreamed it would be. The food was incredible. There weren’t any dietitians or trainers to stop them from eating as much as they wanted. Tula laughed and danced with her best friend from the third Ring all night. They hugged goodbye as Tula held back tears. The girls would have no face-to-face contact with anyone for the next twelve days. Disease prevention, they said.

“What meal would you like after your show?” Her manager’s projected face covered the whole wall. Tula felt cold, so she thought she should order something warm. Then she thought that she should get something big in case she could take the leftovers. As her last meal in the facility, it was her final chance to bring some home.

“A pot of beef stew,” Tula replied. “Must the show be so soon? I fear I’m becoming sick.”

“Sorry, Tula. I don’t get to choose the day,” replied her manager.

A knock came at the door. “Time to leave,” said a gruff voice.

Little Tula looked up at her mother. “Do I have to go?” she asked.

“You’re special, Tula. They’re taking you because you’re the prettiest girl in the sixth Ring,” her mother said with a weak smile.

“Can we have dinner when I get back? My belly is shaking again,” Tula pleaded.

“You’ll get to eat, my little tulip. We all will.” Tula saw her eyes turn red. This confused Tula. Usually her mom was happy when she found them a meal.

Tula walked down the runway with confidence, her countless hours of training taking over. A glass case separated her from the dark spectator room. She stifled a cough as she struck her pose. Tula scanned the audience for her mother. Her manager said Ringers weren’t allowed at the show, but Tula checked anyway just in case. Sometimes Ringers became People. Tula hoped her mother would still recognize her after all this time.

Jack watched his screen as the pale girl stumbled into the quarantine zone. Her skin was snow-white, and bright pink stripes ran up her chest and through her hair. One stripe intersected her left iris, splitting it into white and pink halves. She wore a silly, impractical dress. Blood soaked the front. Jack didn’t understand how anyone could find it beautiful.

“She seems barely conscious, must’ve Bloomed early,” Jack muttered. The showrunners probably cut it extra close with the infection schedule this year. He glanced at his watch. He might make it home for dinner. In a few hours she would Wither, and Jack would suit up. Disinfecting the facility was dangerous. A few cleaners Catch every year. But it paid well enough to keep his family in the Center.

The girl picked up her spoon.

“Beef stew is an odd request, isn’t it? And why a whole pot?” Jack wondered aloud. A broken tulip lay beside it. The girl didn’t look at it. Jack scoffed. “Their attempt at poetry.”

The girl set her spoon down. She hadn’t taken a single bite. She looked directly at the camera. Jack recoiled, her broken eye boring into him.

“Can I take this to go?” she slurred.
